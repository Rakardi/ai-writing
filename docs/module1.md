## Understanding AI Policy

Before you use any AI tool in your work, you need to know whether you are permitted to do so — and under what conditions. This is not bureaucratic caution; it is professional due diligence. AI tools connected to external systems can expose internal data, and the consequences of unauthorized use range from contractual violations to security incidents.

**What an AI policy typically covers:**

- Which AI platforms and models are approved for use.
- What categories of data may or may not be submitted to AI tools (for example, no customer data, no source code, no personally identifiable information).
- Whether outputs generated by AI must be disclosed or labelled.
- Who is accountable when AI-generated content contains errors.
- Whether AI tool usage is logged or audited.

**How to find yours.** Check your company's employee handbook. If you cannot find the relevant information there, ask your line manager.

**If your project does not yet have a policy.** Use your project's existing data handling and confidentiality policies as your guide; default to the most restrictive interpretation. Documenting your own AI usage decisions in the meantime — what tools you used, what data you submitted, and what you verified — is good professional practice and will be valuable if a policy is established later.

## Data Handling in Practice: What to Paste, What Not to Paste

An AI policy tells you the rules. This section tells you how to apply them at the moment you are about to paste text into a prompt — the most common point at which AI mistakes become real incidents.

### Data classification at a glance

Most organizations recognize four broad levels of data sensitivity.

| Level | What it is | Examples in a tech-writing context | Safe to submit to external AI? |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| **Public** | Already published or intended for public release. | Marketing pages, public API docs, published release notes. | Generally yes, consistent with policy. |
| **Internal** | Not secret, but not intended for public distribution. | Drafts of public content, internal style guides, generic templates. | Usually yes on approved platforms; check policy. |
| **Confidential** | Non-public information that would cause harm if disclosed. | Pre-release product details, unreleased roadmap, internal architecture notes, customer names. | Only on enterprise AI tools with a data-processing agreement (DPA). Never on consumer tools. |
| **Restricted** | Legally or contractually protected. | Personally identifiable information (PII), customer data, credentials, source code under NDA, third-party confidential material. | Generally no, regardless of platform. Consult your policy. |

### The "Before You Paste" checklist

Before pasting any text into an AI prompt, ask yourself:

- [ ] Does this contain **personally identifiable information** (names, emails, customer IDs, account numbers)?
- [ ] Does this contain **customer data** or material obtained under an NDA?
- [ ] Does this contain **non-public product information** (unreleased features, roadmap, security details)?
- [ ] Does this contain **credentials, API keys, tokens, or secrets**?
- [ ] Does this contain **third-party confidential material** you don't have permission to share?

If any answer is yes, stop. Either sanitize the content, use an approved internal AI tool with a DPA, or do the task without AI.

### Sanitization techniques

When the content itself is safe but specific details are not, sanitize before submitting:

- **Redaction with placeholders.** Replace specific names, IDs, and secrets with placeholders: `[CUSTOMER_NAME]`, `[API_KEY]`, `[INTERNAL_URL]`. The AI can still reason about structure and wording.
- **Synthetic examples.** For illustrations in documentation, use fabricated data that resembles real data in shape but does not represent any real person, customer, or system.
- **Abstracted scenarios.** Describe the problem at one level of abstraction higher. Instead of "Customer ACME's billing integration with SAP is failing on the Q3 invoice cycle," paste "An enterprise customer's billing integration with their ERP is failing on a quarterly invoice cycle."

### Consumer tools versus enterprise tools

Not all AI tools handle your data the same way.

- **Consumer AI tools** (free or personal-plan chatbots) typically reserve the right to use your inputs for model improvement unless you explicitly opt out. Assume anything you paste may be retained and potentially used in training.
- **Enterprise AI tools with a data-processing agreement (DPA)** typically offer zero-retention inference: your inputs are not stored beyond the session and are not used for training. This is the minimum bar for Confidential content.

If you do not know which tier you are on, ask your support team before pasting anything non-public.

!!! info "The safest habit"

    Sanitize first, then paste. The two seconds it takes to replace a customer name with `[CUSTOMER]` is cheaper than any conversation you will have after a data leak.

## Technical Writers in the World of AI

### Evolving Role

As AI tools mature, most technical writers face three intertwined forces: pressure to adopt, temptation to over-rely, and the potential to elevate their role. Initially, writers who embrace AI gain speed and surface-level efficiency, while skeptics risk falling behind. However, early enthusiasm meets a ceiling—outputs begin to look uniform, mistakes slip through, and the unique value of human writers appears to erode.

A more sustainable model emerges. Writers recognize AI as a powerful collaborator, but not a replacement for human insight. They use AI to automate repetitive or purely structural work, freeing themselves to apply judgment, audience awareness, and creative problem-solving to higher-order tasks. Both risk—job loss from disengagement, and obsolescence from skill atrophy—are real. But the most likely outcome is a hybrid role: technical writers actively integrate AI capabilities, remain vigilant editors and context-setters, and restructure their contributions toward orchestration, quality assurance, and user advocacy.

**Outcome**:
Technical writers who adapt in this hybrid mode avoid obsolescence by balancing augmentation with human oversight. Their value shifts from churning out drafts to curating, validating, and pioneering new standards in technical communication. Organizations that foster this blend reduce risk, harness opportunity, and future-proof both their content and their teams.

## AI Benefits for Technical Writers

Artificial Intelligence (AI) enables machines to mimic human intelligence. It allows systems to learn, reason, and self-correct, replicating capabilities such as visual perception, speech recognition, and decision-making. AI, and specifically Large Language Models (LLMs) within it, have a substantial role to play in technical communication. In this course you will explore AI's potential, its practical applications, and its impact on how technical writers can do their job.

Assisted by AI, a technical writer can:

- *Draw on the massive body of knowledge for research:* dig deep into information provided by AI to contextualize knowledge in the best ways possible.
- *Leverage LLMs for initial drafts and routine tasks:* shift the focus to complex documentation aspects. Use AI to accelerate content creation, keeping pace with the rapid product development cycles.
- *Enhance content quality:* integrate LLMs to improve the clarity, coherence, and engagement of technical documents. This results in higher-quality content that's easier for users to understand and apply.
- *Streamline review processes:* use AI to automate and speed up the review of technical content, identifying inconsistencies or areas that require clarification.
- *Facilitate multilingual documentation:* employ AI to assist with the translation and localization of documents, ensuring that content is accessible to a global audience without losing the technical accuracy.
- *Support creative problem-solving:* ask AI to propose innovative solutions for complex documentation challenges, such as visualizing abstract concepts or creating interactive user guides.
- *Adapt to user feedback:* analyze user feedback through the LLM and incorporate its findings into documentation, continuously improving the relevance and usefulness of the content.

!!! warning "Word of caution"

     While AI is a powerful tool that can augment the technical writer's capabilities, one must approach its output critically. Always check the accuracy and relevance of the information, as AI may not always discern the nuances of context or be aware of the latest industry developments. It is the writer's responsibility to validate the content, ensuring it adheres to the highest standards of veracity, completeness, and ethical practice.

### Key Skills for the AI Era

The following skills matter more than any particular tool. Tools change every quarter; these habits of mind are what make you AI-ready regardless of which platform you end up using.

- **Clear thinking before clear prompting.** You cannot prompt your way out of a confused brief. The clearer your understanding of the reader, the task, and the acceptance criteria, the better every AI interaction will be.
- **Prompt literacy.** Knowing how to structure a prompt — role, context, task, constraints, examples, output format — is now a core writing skill, not a specialist one.
- **Source discipline.** Insisting on verifiable sources for factual claims, and recognizing the difference between an AI that sounds authoritative and an AI that is actually correct.
- **Verification by habit.** Treating every AI output as a draft that must be checked against reality, not a finished product.
- **Workflow thinking.** Seeing documentation as a pipeline with stages, inputs, outputs, and checkpoints — so that AI can be inserted at the right stages and evaluated stage by stage.
- **Ethical awareness.** Understanding the attribution, bias, IP, and confidentiality implications of every AI-assisted deliverable, and choosing accordingly.
- **Continuous recalibration.** The tools and their capabilities shift monthly. The skill is not "learning the tool" but "learning how to keep learning the tool."
